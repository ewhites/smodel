An Introduction to the Standard Model

Hello! Welcome to your SMODEL introductory course of the standard model of particle physics. By selecting non-STEM Undergraduate/High School, you have been placed into the elementary class to introduce particle physics. Before we begin, let us address the content your course will cover.

The purpose of the course is to provide a simple, easy to understand opportunity for curious minds to be introduced to the theory that explains the foundation of our universe. At any point in the course the user is encouraged to access the nomenclature page containing the vocabulary to assist the user during the course.

During your SMODEL course you will receive an introduction to the standard model theory building a foundation of the idea and concept. After understanding the basic concepts of the standard model you will begin section I. Section I will begin your journey by discovering the first of two families in the standard model: the fermions. Jumping into the second section, you will learn about the second family of particles: the bosons. This section will be similar to the first.

The standard model is a theory introduced during the 1970's that provides our best description of the universe on the subatomic scale. The model attempts to connect all particles we have discovered and even predicts some particles we hope to discover in the future.

The standard model consists of 17 main particles. That seems like alot but we will walk you through them all step by step. We will break the particles down into families to help remember and organize the similarities and differences between each, slowly building our knowledge together. The two main families we will divide particles into are the fermions and the bosons. All 17 particles fall into one of these two categories. In fact, these two families are so important we will have a full section on each throughout the course. For now we will leave you with the most important traits of each family. There are 12 fermions and this family of particles contains all mass in the universe. Meanwhile, researchers have discovered 5 bosons. These 5 bosons are the force carriers of the four fundamental forces in the universe. In other words, these particles are the reason that forces exist and we observe interactions because of them.

“Woah hold on… Before we go any further, what was that about the four fundamental forces? That sounds important.” If that was your reaction to the last few lines don’t worry-- that's normal. Before we go any further, let’s introduce these forces-- you may actually know some of them already. The first force is gravity; this is the one we are all familiar with. Gravity pulls us down to earth and causes the earth to orbit the sun. There is one major issue though, gravity is weird, and is the only force that cannot be explained by the standard model. For this reason we will not discuss gravity for the remainder of this course. If you are interested, the more advanced courses will dive into this problem further. Back to the lesson. The second force is the electromagnetic force. The electromagnetic force is what makes currents flow and our lights come on. The final two forces are the weak and strong nuclear forces. We will discuss these forces in this course however once again to learn about what these forces do consider taking the higher level courses after you have completed all the sections we will cover.

OK! So we know there are 17 particles, two majors families, and 4 fundamental forces. What else do we need to cover? This part can be a bit tricky so brace yourself. All particles have a quantity referred to as spin. However, the particles do not actually spin as we think of spinning. The reason this quantity is referred to as spin is because this quantity shares the same units as angular momentum. Because this is a confusing topic, in this course we will refer to spin as quantum strength to make things easier to understand. Every particle has this quantum strength and quantum strength has some hard numbers to memorize. Thankfully physicists have done us a favor. Instead of having this quantum strength value, we can simplify this to a quantum strength number. Each quantum strength number correlates to a value however this makes memorization and classification much easier for everyone. These numbers are quantized in increments of ½. This means possible quantum strength numbers include, 0, 1/2, 1, 3/2, 2, etc…
